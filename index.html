Building a Scale

Scales are the lingua franca of musicians. Knowing which sharps and flats to use helps you set the landscape for musical invention. The basic scales are Major and minor, with minor scales having additional variants of natural minor, harmonic minor, and melodic minor.

The Tetrachord and the Major Scale

The tetrachord is the basic building block of a major scale. As suggested by the root, "tetra," a tetrachord consists of four notes. These notes are spaced apart at a distance of whole step, whole step, half step from the starting note. When played in order, a tetrachord sounds like the theme from The Addams Family.

A major scale is simply two tetrachords joined by a whole step. Experiment with the Scale Builder tool and see if you can figure out the correct notes of different major scales.

The Minor Scale and its Variants

The minor scale has three forms: natural minor, harmonic minor, and melodic minor.

  • Natural Minor can be "related" to the tetrachord-derived major scale. A natural minor scale is the "relative minor" of a major scale when it uses the same notes as the major scale and starts and ends on the sixth scale degree. For example: the relative minor of C major is A natural minor.
  • Harmonic Minor modifies the natural minor scale by raising the seventh scale degree by a half step. This means that the harmonic minor scale will end with a half step. The raised seventh degree introduces the leading tone, which strengthens authentic cadences.
  • Melodic Minor has two parts:
    1. When ascending, raise the sixth and seventh scale degrees.
    2. When descending, return to natural minor.
